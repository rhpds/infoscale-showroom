== Key takeaways

The most important concepts to carry forward from this workshop:

. **DR operations are Kubernetes-native CRDs**: InfoScale exposes all disaster recovery operations -- cluster membership, replication, failover, and failback -- through standard Kubernetes custom resources. This means DR workflows integrate naturally with existing `oc` CLI skills, RBAC, and GitOps pipelines.

. **Replication protects data, not cluster-scoped resources**: The `DataReplication` CRD replicates persistent volume data between clusters. Cluster-scoped resources such as Services and OpenShift Routes are not replicated and must be recreated on the secondary cluster after a failover -- as you did in Module 5.

. **Sequential prerequisites determine DR success**: Each phase of the DR lifecycle builds on the previous one. Skipping verification steps -- such as confirming `Data Status: consistent, up-to-date` before failover, or waiting for `GlobalClusterMembership` to show `CONNECTED` -- leads to incomplete or failed DR operations.

. **Failover and failback use the same mechanism**: The `oc patch` command on the `DisasterRecoveryPlan` drives workload migration in both directions. Changing `primaryCluster` from `cluster-prod` to `cluster-dr` triggers failover; reversing the patch triggers failback. No separate tooling or procedure is required.

. **Data consistency validation closes the DR loop**: Infrastructure checks (pod status, CRD status) confirm the mechanism is working. Application-level validation -- verifying that the James Martinez appointment record was intact after failover -- proves that near-zero data loss through continuous non-periodic replication was achieved and provides the evidence needed for compliance audits.
